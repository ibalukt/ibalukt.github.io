We’ve gathered over 500 active, running roller coasters from around the world, and you can find them all in the expandable table above. Did you know that the tallest roller coaster, the mighty Kingda Ka is over 34 stories high? That’s greater than most radio towers around the world! Looking for more leisurely affair? The little Padrino Voladores barely crests seven miles per hour. Each coaster listed includes their park of residence and the continent you can find it on. In the charts below you’ll find that we’ve broken down our rides into the three hallmarks of any coaster- length, speed and height.

HEIGHT

When we think coasters, we think big. But just how many roller coasters out there really make a climb? Our first chart takes all of our data and splits them up into five ranges. Here, we can see that most of the world’s coasters never get over 90 feet tall. Next, we can see that the top two hundred roller coasters in height find their home in North America. Europe and Asia split evenly and nearly claim the rest. Lastly, when you’re thinking of height, you’re thinking of steel. The vast majority of roller coasters running are made of steel- and they’re even more represented when it comes to height. There tall structures need to be light and flexible in the wind.

LENGTH

Sure, a tall coaster gets the wind in your hair – but what fun is a ride if it’s over quickly? We’ve broken down the track by every 1000th foot. Most coasters sit comfortably in the 1000-200 range. Perhaps unsurprisingly, steel takes an impressive lead once more. Don’t count the classic woodie out though- for over 35 years, King Island’s The Beast has held the record as the longest wooden coaster constructed. It’s so long that it requires two chain-lift drops, and guests will need to hold on for over four minutes. With plenty of space and plenty of parks, North America dominates the scene for the longest super coasters in the world.

SPEED

In the end, it’s all about speed. We tracked from coasters slinking along under 10 miles per hour to blistering speeds over 121 miles per hour. Most roller coasters, sit comfortably in between; around 40-60 miles per hour. You might be seeing a trend here – steel coasters take the lead once more. Notably however, while many small steel coasters populate the lower speed ranges, most wooden coasters are sizable projects – and fast. As in all other categories, North America tops the list for the top two hundred fastest roller coasters.
